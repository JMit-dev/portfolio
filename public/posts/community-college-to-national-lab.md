I enrolled at Suffolk County Community College in 2022 with a computer science degree in mind and no particular plan beyond that. Two years later I was presenting research at Brookhaven National Laboratory. A year after that I was presenting at an international physics conference.

I'm not telling this story to be impressive. I'm telling it because when I was at SCCC I had no idea that path was available to me, and I think more community college students should know it is.

## The Assumption That Kills Opportunities

The dominant narrative about research internships, especially at places like national labs, is that they're for students at research universities with existing lab connections, strong GPAs from selective schools, and advisors who know how to navigate the application process.

This is partly true and mostly a reason not to give up.

The DOE national lab system has programs specifically designed to reach students who don't have those advantages. The CCI (Community College Internships) program is the most direct example — it's the same application process as SULI (the undergraduate internship program), the same research environment, the same stipend, specifically for community college students.

I applied to CCI during my first year at SCCC. I applied because a professor mentioned it in passing, I looked it up, and it seemed like something worth trying. The downside of applying was the time to fill out an application. The upside was a research position at a DOE national lab. That's not a hard calculation.

## The Application

The application is through the ORISE (Oak Ridge Institute for Science and Education) portal. You rank your preferred labs, write a personal statement, submit transcripts, and get one recommendation from a faculty member.

The personal statement is where most people lose. Generic statements — "I am passionate about science and eager to learn" — don't work. Specific statements do.

For my CCI application I wrote about the specific type of work BNL's Instrumentation Division was doing, what I already knew about control systems and software, and what I wanted to learn. I wrote about why I was interested in scientific computing rather than software engineering broadly. I connected my coursework to the lab's actual research.

That specificity matters because the person reading your application is a researcher, not an HR screener. They're looking for someone who actually understands what they're walking into.

## The Gap Between School and Research

The biggest adjustment from community college coursework to research is the ambiguity.

In a class, you're given a problem with a known answer. The rubric tells you what success looks like. You produce the expected output and get graded.

In research, you're given a direction and a goal. Nobody knows exactly what the solution looks like because if they did, they wouldn't need you to find it. You might spend a week going down a path that turns out to be wrong. You might discover the obvious approach doesn't work and have to go find a non-obvious one. This is normal and expected.

The students who struggle are the ones who wait for permission to proceed or who interpret uncertainty as failure. The ones who do well are the ones who treat the unclear path as the job — figure out what you don't know, try things, document what happens, ask specific questions when genuinely stuck.

This is a learnable skill but it's not taught in most coursework. The earlier you can get research exposure, the faster you develop it.

## What the CC to BNL Path Actually Looked Like

- **Fall 2022**: Started SCCC. Applied for CCI on a whim after a professor mentioned it.
- **Spring 2023**: Worked as undergraduate researcher at SCCC on a water quality project. Presented at SURC (SUNY Undergraduate Research Conference). Got some basic research experience.
- **Summer 2023**: Got the CCI offer. Spent the summer at BNL in the Instrumentation Division. Built control system software. Presented at the fall symposium.
- **Fall 2023**: Started tutoring CS at SCCC. Applied for SULI.
- **Summer 2025**: SULI at BNL. Bigger project, more independence, same facility.
- **Fall 2025**: Presented the SULI work at ICALEPCS 2025.
- **November 2025**: Hired as Student Assistant at BNL to continue the work.

The CCI internship opened the door. Everything after it was a direct consequence of that first internship — connections made, work demonstrated, familiarity built.

## What I'd Tell My 2022 Self

**Apply anyway.** The worst-case scenario of applying and not getting it is identical to not applying. The best case is a research position at a national lab. Apply.

**Find the programs specifically for your situation.** CCI exists. NSF REU programs exist. State-level programs exist. Most community college students don't know about them because the information flow goes through advisors and professors who are sometimes as unaware as the students. Do your own research.

**Your GPA matters but it's not everything.** Particularly for technical programs, demonstrated interest and specific knowledge in the research area carry weight. I had a strong GPA at SCCC but I also wrote a personal statement that showed I'd actually read what the lab was doing.

**Research experience compounds.** The first opportunity is the hardest to get. After that, each experience makes the next one easier to obtain and more productive once you're in it.

Community college is a legitimate starting point. Not a consolation prize, not a detour — a starting point. The path from there to wherever you want to go exists. Find it.
