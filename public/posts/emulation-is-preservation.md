Let's get the obvious argument out of the way first: the games industry calls emulation piracy. Nintendo has sued emulator developers. Sony shuts down storefronts and then acts surprised when people turn to alternative means to play their old games. The official position is that running a ROM of a game you own on software that mimics the original hardware is theft.

This position is legally contested, morally bankrupt, and historically illiterate.

## What Emulation Actually Is

An emulator is software that accurately replicates the behavior of a piece of hardware. A Game Boy emulator implements the Sharp LR35902 CPU, the PPU, the memory map, the timer, the audio channels — everything that the physical hardware does, in software. When you run a ROM through it, you get an experience functionally identical to what the original cartridge produces on original hardware.

This is an engineering achievement, not a crime. The people who write emulators are often the most knowledgeable about the hardware they're emulating. The MAME project has documented more about arcade hardware than any official source. The melonDS developers understand the DS's dual-CPU architecture better than most Nintendo engineers working today.

## What "Preservation" Actually Means

Gaming is less than 60 years old and we've already lost enormous amounts of it.

Magnetic media degrades. Cartridge batteries die. The chemicals in optical discs break down. Flash storage corrupts. Companies go under and take their catalogs with them. Servers shut off and kill digital games. The Sony PSP Go's digital storefront is gone. The DSi Shop is gone. The Wii Shop is gone. The Wii U eShop is gone. The 3DS eShop closed in 2023.

When a game's only legal distribution method disappears, emulation becomes the only way it exists.

Cities have libraries. Museums have archivists. Historians have archives. Film has the Library of Congress. Gaming has none of these infrastructure systems at scale. What it has is a community of people dumping cartridges, preserving ROMs, writing emulators, and documenting hardware — almost entirely in their spare time, without compensation, against the legal threats of the very companies whose products they're saving.

The Internet Archive. No-Intro. Redump. TOSEC. These are the Library of Congress of gaming, built by hobbyists. Without them, Earthbound would exist only in physical cartridges slowly failing on shelves.

## Nintendo's Position Is Incoherent

Nintendo is the loudest voice against emulation. They've also failed more comprehensively than anyone to make their own catalog accessible.

Where do you buy Super Mario RPG for the SNES? You can get a modern remake. The original? Nowhere legally. Where do you buy most of the Game Boy library? Nowhere. Fire Emblem: Genealogy of the Holy War? Mother 3? The original Metroid II? Nowhere, nowhere, nowhere.

Nintendo releases a curated handful of games on Nintendo Switch Online, at their discretion, on a subscription that could vanish. The rest of their history — thousands of games across NES, SNES, N64, Game Boy, GBA, DS, Wii, and Wii U — is simply unavailable. They don't sell it. They don't license it. They just sit on it and sue anyone who tries to fill the gap.

This is not a company that cares about preservation. This is a company that wants to control the terms under which you access entertainment, forever, even when they've actively chosen not to make it available at any price.

## The Legal Reality

In the United States, the DMCA has a preservation exemption. Libraries and archives can legally circumvent copy protection for preservation purposes. Dumping your own cartridges to ROM format is broadly considered legal in most jurisdictions. Running a ROM of a game you own is legally murky but defensible.

Emulator software itself — the code that mimics hardware — has been ruled legal in multiple cases. The 1992 *Sega v. Accolade* ruling established that reverse engineering for interoperability is fair use. The 2000 *Sony v. Connectix* ruling found that creating a PlayStation emulator through clean-room reverse engineering was legal.

The legal grey area is ROMs you didn't dump yourself. But the moral argument is separate from the legal one, and the moral argument is straightforward: if a publisher has abandoned a work, made no effort to make it available, and threatens legal action against those who preserve it, the ethical weight is not on the side of the publisher.

## What I Actually Do

I build emulators. My [i8080 Space Invaders emulator](https://github.com/JMit-dev/i8080-spaceinvaders-emulator) implements the Intel 8080 CPU at a cycle-accurate level. My [CHIP-8 emulator](https://jmit-dev.github.io/chip8-tutorial/) is built specifically to teach how emulation works, with live visualization of registers and memory.

I build these because the hardware is interesting, the engineering challenge is real, and because I believe software that runs on a chip deserves to be understood, documented, and kept alive by people who care about it — regardless of whether a corporation decides it's commercially convenient.

Emulation is how gaming history survives. It's an act of care for a medium that the industry treats as disposable.
