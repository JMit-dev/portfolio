I've done multiple research internships at this point and I've watched other interns struggle with things that were genuinely hard to anticipate. Most of the difficulty isn't technical — it's about understanding the environment you're in and what success actually looks like there.

Here's what I'd tell someone about to start their first research internship, particularly in a scientific computing or controls role at a national lab.

## Nobody Will Tell You What to Do Every Day

The most common failure mode I've seen in research interns is waiting. Waiting for the mentor to give them the next task. Waiting for someone to check in. Waiting to be told it's okay to start.

Research doesn't work this way. Your mentor has their own projects, meetings, and responsibilities. They gave you a goal and some context and they expect you to operate with significant autonomy between check-ins. If you're blocked, the expectation is usually that you've tried several things and come with specific questions — not that you've been sitting idle until someone asks.

The adjustment from coursework (where the path is always clear and help is always available) to research (where the path is often unclear and you're expected to navigate it yourself) is the biggest thing to internalize. Treat the ambiguity as your job. You're being paid to figure things out.

## Read Everything That Exists First

Before you write a line of code, read everything you can find about the system you're working on. Documentation, README files, previous intern reports, design documents, papers, talks. This is time well spent even when it feels like you're not making progress.

At BNL, my first week was mostly reading. I read the Phoebus documentation, the Bluesky documentation, the EPICS application developer guide, the previous intern's project report, and every relevant GitHub repository. By the time I started writing code I had a mental model of where everything fit. That saved enormous amounts of time later.

People who skip this and dive straight into code spend weeks debugging things the documentation explicitly explains.

## Ask Questions Strategically

There's a wrong way and a right way to ask for help in a research environment.

Wrong: "I'm stuck. What should I do?"

Right: "I'm trying to do X. I've tried Y and Z. Y failed because [specific error]. Z got further but hit [specific issue]. My current hypothesis is [hypothesis]. Does that make sense or am I missing something?"

The second version shows you've thought about the problem, tried things, and formed a theory. It makes the conversation productive. It also demonstrates the kind of thinking that makes a mentor want to continue investing in you.

Mentors are not TAs. They don't have office hours. They're researchers who've taken on an intern because they think the intern can contribute. Respecting their time by coming prepared is not just courtesy — it's how you get the most out of the relationship.

## Documentation Is Part of the Work

Write things down as you go. Document what you build, why you made the decisions you made, and how someone else would run or modify it. This is not optional busywork — it's a core part of delivering something useful.

Research code that isn't documented is code that will be abandoned or rewritten by the next person. Code that's documented well can be built upon. If you're building something that people will use after you leave, documentation is part of what you're delivering.

At the end of a research internship, you usually write a final report and give a presentation. The quality of those outputs depends heavily on having kept notes throughout. Start a running document on day one and add to it whenever you do something interesting or make a decision worth recording.

## The Poster Presentation Is Important

Most research internship programs culminate in a poster presentation. A lot of interns treat this as administrative overhead. It isn't.

The poster presentation is the primary external artifact that communicates what you did. It's what your mentor's colleagues see. It's what goes in the program record. It's often what determines whether you get invited back, recommended to others, or asked to continue the work.

Start working on it earlier than feels necessary. The constraint of fitting your work onto a poster forces you to understand what was actually important about it. If you can't explain your project in 3-4 sentences to someone who doesn't know the field, you don't understand it clearly enough yet.

I've presented at BNL symposiums and at ICALEPCS 2025. The practice of explaining technical work clearly to a mixed audience is genuinely valuable — it's a skill that transfers everywhere.

## Manage Your Own Time

Nobody is tracking your hours closely. Nobody is checking that you're at your desk. This is not permission to coast — it's a different kind of accountability. You're accountable to the outcome, not the process.

Set weekly goals for yourself at the start of each week. At the end of each week, write down what you actually accomplished versus what you planned. This keeps you honest about pace and catches problems before they become crises at the end.

If you're falling behind, tell your mentor early. "I thought this would take two weeks but it's taking longer because of X" is a solvable problem. Discovering at week 8 that you built the wrong thing is much harder.

## The Technical Skills Are Table Stakes

You were hired because you have relevant technical skills. Those skills are necessary but they're not what distinguishes a good research intern from a mediocre one.

What distinguishes a good intern is judgment (knowing what to build and what to skip), communication (explaining what you built and why), and reliability (doing what you said you'd do). Technical skill is assumed. Everything else is what you actually get evaluated on.

Go in prepared. Be curious. Take the work seriously. Ask good questions. Write things down. Those things matter more than any specific technology you know.
