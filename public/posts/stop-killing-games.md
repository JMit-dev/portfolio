In April 2024, Ubisoft shut down *The Crew* — a racing game released in 2014 that sold millions of copies. They didn't just shut down the servers. They deleted the game from players' libraries. People who paid $60 for it could no longer launch it at all. The game was, functionally, destroyed.

This is what *Stop Killing Games* is about.

## The Problem

Modern games — especially live service games — are built to require a connection to publisher servers to function. Some do this for multiplayer. Many do it for DRM. Some do it simply because they were designed that way. When a publisher decides the game is no longer profitable enough to maintain, they shut down those servers, and the game ceases to exist.

You don't get a refund. You don't get an offline version. You don't get a warning with enough time to prepare. You just lose what you paid for.

This has happened to hundreds of games. *Knockout City*. *Babylon's Fall*. *Marvel Avengers*. *Hyper Scape*. *Anthem* nearly. *Concord* — dead in two weeks. Each time, every copy purchased is retroactively destroyed. Not discontinued. Destroyed.

## The Campaign

[Stop Killing Games](https://www.stopkillinggames.com/) is a consumer rights initiative started by YouTuber Ross Scott (Freeman's Mind, Accursed Farms) that's pushing for legislation to prevent this.

The specific ask is reasonable: when a publisher decides to end support for a game, they must release it in a form that can be run without their servers. This doesn't mean open sourcing the entire engine. It means providing players what they need to continue running what they already paid for — an offline mode, server emulator tools, or a modified client that doesn't require authentication.

The campaign launched a European Citizens' Initiative that collected over a million signatures across EU member states, triggering a formal requirement for the European Commission to respond. As of 2025, this is an active legislative discussion in Europe.

## Why This Is a Consumer Rights Issue

When you buy a physical book, it doesn't stop working because the publisher goes under. When you buy a car, Ford can't remotely disable it because they decided your model is end-of-life. When you buy a piece of software on physical media, you own that media.

Digital distribution has systematically eroded this. The EULAs that nobody reads but everyone agrees to contain language saying you're "licensing" the game rather than buying it. Publishers have used this to argue they owe you nothing when they destroy what you paid for.

Stop Killing Games argues — correctly — that regardless of what a EULA says, selling someone a product that is deliberately designed to stop working is a form of fraud. Consumer protection law in most jurisdictions doesn't allow you to sell something and then remotely destroy it. The games industry has been getting away with this because legislators haven't caught up.

## The Counter-Arguments Are Weak

**"It's too expensive to maintain old games."** The ask isn't to maintain them. It's to release them in a form that runs without your servers when you stop maintaining them. These are different things.

**"It would expose their trade secrets."** A server emulator or offline patch doesn't require releasing source code. The technical implementation can be protected while still allowing players to run what they paid for.

**"Nobody plays these old games anyway."** *The Crew* had an active player base when it was shut down. But even if it didn't — you still sold it. The sale happened. The obligation exists regardless of current player counts.

**"This would prevent innovation in live service games."** No. It would prevent publishers from using live service architecture as a mechanism to avoid consumer protection obligations. You can make a live service game. You just have to ensure it can outlive your commercial interest in it.

## What You Can Do

- Sign the [European Citizens' Initiative](https://www.stopkillinggames.com/) if you're an EU citizen
- Sign the petition regardless — international numbers matter for the broader argument
- Stop buying live service games from publishers with a history of shutting them down prematurely
- Talk about this — most people don't know this is happening

I support Stop Killing Games because I believe software you paid for should remain yours. Not licensed to you at the publisher's discretion. Yours. The campaign isn't asking for much. It's asking for what we already expect from every other consumer product.
