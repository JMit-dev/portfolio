Steam has 50,000 games. The PlayStation Store has thousands more. You can buy almost anything instantly, it downloads in minutes, and you never have to find shelf space for another case. Digital distribution is genuinely convenient, and I use it.

It is also a format where you own nothing and can lose everything without warning.

## What "Owning" a Digital Game Actually Means

When you buy a game on Steam, you are purchasing a license to access that software under Valve's terms of service, which Valve can change, revoke, or terminate at any time. Your Steam library is not an asset. It's a revocable permission slip.

The same is true for PlayStation, Xbox, Nintendo, Epic, GOG (to varying degrees), and every other digital storefront. You are not buying software. You are renting access to it for an undefined period, with the publisher and platform holding all the termination rights.

This is not hypothetical. People have been banned from PSN and permanently lost libraries worth thousands of dollars. Games have been delisted from storefronts — *P.T.*, *Scott Pilgrim vs. the World*, hundreds of licensed games when the license expired — and anyone who didn't already own them simply cannot buy them again. Games you own can be patched into different products, have content removed, or have features stripped.

With a physical cartridge or disc, none of this applies. The software is on the media. It runs without permission from anyone. It will run in 30 years the same way it runs today.

## The Storefront Graveyard

Digital storefronts have a poor survival record:

- **GameSpy** — shut down 2014, killed online for hundreds of PC games
- **Games for Windows Live** — shut down 2013
- **Wii Shop Channel** — shut down 2019
- **DSi Shop** — shut down 2017
- **3DS/Wii U eShop** — closed 2023
- **PlayStation Store on PS3/Vita** — nearly closed 2021, partially saved by backlash
- **Google Stadia** — shut down 2023, games gone
- **EA Origin (replaced)** — migrated but library management remains precarious

Every one of these represented games that players paid for that became inaccessible. Some were partially mitigated. Many weren't. Google refunded Stadia purchases — eventually, under pressure — but there's no legal requirement to do so.

The question is not whether digital storefronts close. They do. The question is what happens to your library when they do.

## Physical Media in 2025

The argument against physical media is usually "inconvenient" or "takes up space." Both are true. Neither changes the fundamental ownership picture.

A Game Boy cartridge from 1990 runs today without connecting to any server, without any authentication, without any ongoing relationship with Nintendo. The software on that cartridge is yours in a way that no digital purchase replicates. You can lend it. You can sell it. You can give it to someone else. You can play it on original hardware or an emulator. You are in complete control.

Modern physical media has eroded some of this — day-one patches, online passes, games that are mostly a disc that triggers a download — but even a partially physical product is more durable than a purely digital one. The disc provides a fallback. A digital purchase provides none.

## The Resale Argument

Physical games have a resale market. You can sell a game you finished, recover some of what you spent, and use it toward the next purchase. You can buy used games at significant discounts. Publishers hate this because they make no money on secondary sales, which is why they've aggressively pushed digital — digital has no resale market by design.

The used game market is good for consumers. It provides a price floor, a way to access older games affordably, and a mechanism that treats games as durable goods rather than consumable services. Digital eliminates all of this.

## What I Do

I buy physical where it exists and matters. For older consoles especially — PS1, PS2, DS, 3DS, Game Boy — physical is the only format that makes sense. The software will outlive any service.

For modern games, I'm increasingly selective about digital purchases. Games that require no DRM or server authentication (many indie games, most GOG titles) are relatively safe. Games tied to live services or launcher accounts are a bet that the platform survives and stays accessible indefinitely. That's a bet I'm less and less willing to make.

The convenience of digital is real. The cost of that convenience is permanent, structural dependence on companies to continue allowing you access to what you paid for. For me, that's a cost I try to avoid where possible. Keep what you buy. Buy physical when you can.
