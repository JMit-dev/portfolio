## What Is a DSi LL?

The DSi LL (called the DSi XL in North America) is Nintendo's large-format version of the DSi,
released in Japan in 2009. It plays every DS and DSi game, has the biggest screens ever put on a
DS-family handheld, and it's built like a tank. The Japanese model specifically is relevant
because of one thing: price.

Import a Japanese DSi LL and you'll typically pay $30-50 in good condition. The North American
DSi XL runs $70-120 for the same hardware. They are functionally identical. The Japanese model
has a menu in Japanese, but you won't be seeing that menu for long.

## The Display Is the Best Way to Play DS Games. Period.

This is not up for debate. The DSi LL has the largest, highest-quality screens of any DS
hardware. The pixel density is lower than the original DS screens, which means pixels are
visible — and on DS games, this actually looks *correct*. DS games were designed for screens
at this size and viewing distance. The art direction on games like *Radiant Historia*, 
*Strange Journey*, or *Castlevania: Dawn of Sorrow* was made to be seen this way.

Emulation on modern hardware applies scaling algorithms, integer scaling, or bilinear filtering
that smears or misrepresents the original image. Even the best DS emulator on the best hardware
is approximating what a DSi LL does natively. If you care about playing these games as they
were designed to be experienced, original hardware on the DSi LL is the answer.

The screens are also backlit and bright. The viewing angles are wide. The form factor is
comfortable for adult hands in a way the original DS Lite is not. Nintendo never made a better
handheld for actually sitting down and playing DS games.

## Modding It Is Trivial

Here's what you do after the DSi LL arrives:

**1. Install Unlaunch**

Unlaunch is a bootloader that runs before the DSi's firmware. You install it once through
the DSi's browser exploit. After that you have permanent, full control of the hardware. It
survives system updates and cannot be removed by Nintendo remotely. This takes about 10 minutes.

**2. Install TWiLight Menu++**

TWiLight Menu++ is an open-source frontend that runs DS and DSi ROMs from your SD card. It
replaces the DSi menu entirely with a clean interface that lets you browse and launch games.
Install it to your SD card, drop ROMs in a folder, done.

**3. Fix the Locale**

Japanese DSi LLs default to Japanese in the system settings. Set the language to English in
the console settings and TWiLight Menu++ will follow. Everything will be in English. The
Japanese origin of the hardware becomes irrelevant the moment you boot a game.

The whole setup takes under an hour. You don't need any special hardware, soldering, or
technical knowledge beyond following a guide. The [DSi Guide](https://dsi.cfw.guide/) walks
you through every step.

## Nintendo Will Not Sell You These Games

Here's the part that makes me angry.

The DS has over 1,800 games. The DSi had a digital storefront called the DSi Shop that sold
DSiWare titles. Nintendo closed that shop in 2017. The Wii U Virtual Console, which had a
handful of DS games, is now also dead. The 3DS eShop closed in 2023.

There is no legal way to purchase the majority of DS games digitally in 2026. Nintendo has
made no effort to bring the DS catalog to Switch, Switch 2, or any modern platform. Games
like *Radiant Historia*, *Trauma Center*, *The World Ends With You* (the original, not the
overpriced Switch port), *Solatorobo*, *Kirby: Mass Attack*, *Ghost Trick* — the list of
games you simply cannot buy from Nintendo goes on for hundreds of entries.

Nintendo's official position, stated repeatedly through legal action and lobbying, is that
piracy is theft. Their actual behavior is to let an entire generation of games disappear
while suing anyone who tries to preserve them. The DSi LL mod scene exists because Nintendo
left a vacuum. Filling that vacuum is the correct response.

If these games were available to purchase, I would purchase them. They are not. The publisher
has abandoned them. Playing them on modded hardware is preservation.

Digital games that vanish when storefronts close. Physical media that increasingly requires
online verification. Prices that have crept to $80 per title. A used game market they've

I support [Stop Killing Games](https://www.stopkillinggames.com/). I support the right to
repair what you own. The DSi LL mod scene is a direct expression of both principles: taking
hardware you purchased, running software on it you choose, preserving games the publisher
has made unavailable, and extending the life of a device indefinitely with community-developed
tools.

The DSi LL will play DS games correctly in 2026. It will play them correctly in 2036. No
server needs to be online. No license needs to be validated. No storefront needs to remain
open. You own the hardware, you own the SD card, you have the games.

That's what owning something actually looks like.
